# Introduction

# Where does the Biharmonic equation come from?

Before tackling the Biharmonic equation, first we will briefly consider some
application where the Biharmonic equation arises.

## Stokes flow

In the low Reynolds number limit, the Navier-Stokes equations reduce to the
Stokes equations, given by

$$
\begin{align}
    \nabla^2 \mathbf{u} &= \nabla p, \\
    \nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} &= 0.
\end{align}
$$

For a two-dimensional flow,
the second equation implies the existence of a stream function $\psi$ such that

$$
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{u} &= \nabla\times(\psi \hat{\mathbf{z}})\\
    &= \begin{pmatrix}
        \partial_y \psi \\
        -\partial_x \psi
    \end{pmatrix}.
\end{align}
$$

It is clear to see that under this definition, the velocity field $\mathbf{u}$ is
automatically incompressible.
We can then rewrite the first equation as

$$
\begin{align}
    \nabla^2(\nabla \times \psi \hat{\mathbf{z}}) &= \nabla p,
\end{align}
$$

or in component form,

$$
\begin{equation}
\begin{pmatrix}
    \psi_{xxy} + \psi_{yyy} \\
    -\psi_{xxx} - \psi_{xyy}
\end{pmatrix} =
\begin{pmatrix}
    p_x \\
    p_y
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
$$

Taking the curl of both sides (noting the curl of a gradient is zero) gives

$$
\begin{align}
    \psi_{xxxx} + 2\psi_{xxyy} + \psi_{yyyy} &= 0,
\end{align}
$$

which is the biharmonic equation.

## Linear Elasticity

In linear elasticity, the displacement field $\mathbf{u}$ satisfies the Navier-Cauchy equations,

$$
\begin{align}
    \rho \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{u}}{\partial t^2} = \mu \nabla^2 \mathbf{u} + (\lambda + \mu) \nabla (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u}) + \mathbf{f},
\end{align}
$$

where $\lambda$ and $\mu$ are the Lamé parameters, $\rho$ is the density, and $\mathbf{f}$ is the body force.
In the absence of body forces and for static equilibrium, the equation reduces to

$$
\begin{align}
    \mu \nabla^2 \mathbf{u} + (\lambda + \mu) \nabla (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u}) = 0.
\end{align}
$$

If we consider the case of plane strain, that is,

$$
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{u} = (u(x,y), v(x,y), 0),
\end{equation}
$$
