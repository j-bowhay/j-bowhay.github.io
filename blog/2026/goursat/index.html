The Goursat representation of the biharmonic equation

Created on March 20, 2026

2026   ·   complex-analysis   continuum-mechanics

Warning: these notes are work in progress.

Introduction

This note concerns the Biharmonic equation, which is a fourth-order, elliptic partial differential equation given by

\[\begin{align} \nabla^4 \psi = 0, \end{align}\]

where $\nabla^4$ is the biharmonic operator, defined as $\nabla^4 = \nabla^2 \nabla^2$. The biharmonic equation is also sometime known as the Bilaplacian. More specifically, we will explore how complex variables can be used to solve the biharmonic equation in two dimensions.

Where does the Biharmonic equation come from?

Before tackling the Biharmonic equation, first we will briefly consider some application where the Biharmonic equation arises.

Stokes flow

In the low Reynolds number limit, the Navier-Stokes equations reduce to the Stokes equations, given by

\[\begin{align} \nabla^2 \mathbf{u} &= \nabla p, \\ \nabla \cdot \mathbf{u} &= 0. \end{align}\]

For a two-dimensional flow, the second equation implies the existence of a stream function $\psi$ such that

\[\begin{align} \mathbf{u} &= \nabla\times(\psi \hat{\mathbf{z}})\\ &= \begin{pmatrix} \partial_y \psi \\ -\partial_x \psi \end{pmatrix}. \end{align}\]

It is clear to see that under this definition, the velocity field $\mathbf{u}$ is automatically incompressible. We can then rewrite the first equation as

\[\begin{align} \nabla^2(\nabla \times \psi \hat{\mathbf{z}}) &= \nabla p, \end{align}\]

or in component form,

\[\begin{equation} \begin{pmatrix} \psi_{xxy} + \psi_{yyy} \\ -\psi_{xxx} - \psi_{xyy} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} p_x \\ p_y \end{pmatrix}. \end{equation}\]

Taking the curl of both sides (noting the curl of a gradient is zero) gives

\[\begin{align} \psi_{xxxx} + 2\psi_{xxyy} + \psi_{yyyy} &= 0, \end{align}\]

which is the biharmonic equation.

Linear Elasticity

In linear elasticity, the displacement field $\mathbf{u}$ satisfies the Navier-Cauchy equations,

\[\begin{align} \rho \frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{u}}{\partial t^2} = \mu \nabla^2 \mathbf{u} + (\lambda + \mu) \nabla (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u}) + \mathbf{f}, \end{align}\]

where $\lambda$ and $\mu$ are the Lamé parameters, $\rho$ is the density, and $\mathbf{f}$ is the body force. In the absence of body forces and for static equilibrium, the equation reduces to

\[\begin{align} \mu \nabla^2 \mathbf{u} + (\lambda + \mu) \nabla (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{u}) = 0. \end{align}\]

If we consider the case of plane strain, that is,

\[\begin{equation} \mathbf{u} = (u(x,y), v(x,y), 0), \end{equation}\]

Wirtinger calculus

Before we can tackle deriving the Goursat representation of the biharmonic equation, we first need some machinery from complex analysis, known as the Wirtinger calculus.

Given a complex variable $z = x + iy$, its complex conjugate $\bar{z} = x - iy$, and a complex-valued function $f(x,y)$, can consider the change of variables from $(x,y)$ to $(z, \bar{z})$. Since, $x = (z + \bar{z})$/2 and $y = (z - \bar{z})/2i$, we can define $\tilde{f}(z, \bar{z}) = f((z + \bar{z})/2, (z - \bar{z})/2i)$. By the chain rule, we then have that

\[\begin{align} \frac{\partial \tilde{f}}{\partial z} &= \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \frac{\partial x}{\partial z} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \frac{\partial y}{\partial z} \\ &= \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} - i \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \right) \\ \frac{\partial \tilde{f}}{\partial \bar{z}} &= \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \frac{\partial x}{\partial \bar{z}} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \frac{\partial y}{\partial \bar{z}} \\ &= \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} + i \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \right). \end{align}\]

At first this seems a little bit strange, as $z$ and $\bar{z}$ are clearly not independent variables, but this is irrelevant as $x$ and $y$ are the fundamental input variables. Henceforth, we will abuse notation and simply write $f$ regardless of whether we are considering $f$ as a function of $(x,y)$ or $(z, \bar{z})$.

Note that, if $f$ is a holomorphic function, then $\partial f / \partial \bar{z} = 0$, and $\partial f / \partial z$ is the usual complex derivative of $f$.

Goursat representation

Finally, we are ready to derive the Goursat representation of the biharmonic equation. Using the Wirtinger calculus, we can rewrite the biharmonic equation as

\[\begin{align} \frac{\partial^4 \psi}{\partial z^2 \partial \bar{z}^2} = 0. \end{align}\]
